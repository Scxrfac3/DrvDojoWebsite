100% Independent

CHAPTER 02

What Is an ADI?

An Approved Driving Instructor (ADI) is the only legally recognised qualification that allows you to charge money for driving lessons in the UK.

You cannot legally take payment for teaching someone to drive without it.

DVSA Regulated

Administered by the Driver and Vehicle Standards Agency

Three-Part Process

Must pass all three examinations to qualify

Green Badge

Display your registration in your vehicle at all times

The Three Parts — Overview

Each part tests a different skill set. Most candidates take 9–18 months from start to badge, though it's possible to complete faster with focused effort.

1

Part 1 — Theory & Hazard Awareness

A knowledge-based computer test covering teaching knowledge, road safety, and hazard perception. Duration: 90 minutes.

90 min ~£100 Pass rate: ~50%
2

Part 2 — Driving Ability

Your own driving tested to an advanced standard. Must demonstrate skill significantly above a standard licence holder.

~60 min ~£100 Pass rate: ~45%
3

Part 3 — Instructional Ability

Two 30-minute practical lessons where you teach the examiner (playing the role of a pupil). This is where 70% of candidates fail.

60 min ~£100 Pass rate: ~30%
DRIVE DOJO TIP

My strategy: I completed Part 1 while studying for my university exams — it's all knowledge-based. Then I used the university summer break to intensively train for Part 2 and 3. Total time from start to qualified: 11 months.

CHAPTER 03

Part 1 — Theory Test

Part 1 is a knowledge test — and most candidates underestimate it severely.

Unlike the standard learner theory test, the ADI Part 1 includes questions specifically about teaching methodology, learning theory, and road safety statistics. This is not a test of your driving knowledge — it's a test of whether you understand how to teach.

What the Test Covers

Teaching Theory

Learning styles, teaching methods, communication techniques

Road Safety

Statistics, safety measures, hazard awareness

Legal Requirements

Theory of instruction, DVSA regulations

Hazard Perception

Video-based hazard identification clips

8-Week Study Plan

I designed this plan based on how I passed Part 1 while studying for my Computer Science degree. It requires 1-2 hours per day of focused study.

WEEK 1-2

Foundation

  • Purchase study materials (see below)
  • Read through all topic areas once
  • Focus on teaching theory modules
  • 1hr daily revision minimum
WEEK 3-4

Deep Dive

  • Start hazard perception practice
  • Focus on road safety statistics
  • Begin mock tests (score target: 80%+)
  • 1.5hr daily revision
WEEK 5-6

Intensive

  • Daily mock tests (aim for 85%+ consistently)
  • Review incorrect answers thoroughly
  • Practice hazard perception clips daily
  • 2hr daily revision
WEEK 7-8

Final Push

  • Book your test when scoring 90%+
  • Final mock test marathon
  • Review weak areas only
  • 1hr daily to maintain freshness

CHAPTER 04

Part 2 — Driving Ability

Part 2 tests your own driving to an advanced standard — and it's harder than most people expect.

The examiner is looking for skill significantly above what you'd need for a standard driving licence. This is about defensive driving, spatial awareness, and anticipating hazards before they develop.

What Examiners Look For

Observation

Mirror checks, blind spots, anticipating other road users

Speed Control

Appropriate speeds, smooth acceleration and braking

Road Position

Correct lane position, roundabout procedures

Commentary

Continuous verbal commentary on what you see and anticipate

Common Reasons for Failure

Insufficient Observation

Not checking mirrors at junctions or before changing direction

Following Too Close

Tailgating in traffic — maintaining safe distances

Hesitation

Being overly cautious at roundabouts and junctions

Poor Commentary

Stopping the commentary or not being anticipatory

Positioning Errors

Incorrect lane position on bends or at junctions

Traffic Lights

Approaching signals too fast or blocking box junctions

The Independent Training Route

You do NOT need to pay a franchise thousands of pounds. Here's how I trained for Part 2:

Join IAM RoadSmart or RoSPA

Advanced driving coaching from £150-400. Much cheaper than franchise training and teaches you to an equivalent or higher standard.

IAM RoadSmart: ~£200 RoSPA: ~£350

Book 4-6 Targeted Lessons

Find a qualified ADI who offers Part 2 preparation lessons in your area. Ask in Facebook groups for recommendations.

Record & Review

Use a dashcam to record your drives. Review footage to identify bad habits and improvement areas.

Roadcraft Handbook

The police-approved gold standard for advanced driving technique. Essential reading.

DRIVE DOJO TIP

What actually worked for me: I joined IAM RoadSmart for £195 and took 5 advanced driving lessons with an IAM observer. The total cost was under £400. My Part 2 pass came on the first attempt. Don't let anyone tell you need to spend franchise money.

CHAPTER 05

Part 3 — Instructional Ability

Part 3 is where the majority of candidates fail — up to 70% on first attempt.

This is not a test of your driving — it's a test of your ability to teach. The examiner plays the role of a nervous learner. You must demonstrate genuine pupil-centred learning.

How the Exam Works

2
30-Minute Lessons
60
Minutes Total
4
Competency Areas

What Examiners Mark (LAMS Scale):

  • 1. Planning & Problem Solving

    Can you identify pupil needs and create a structured lesson plan?

  • 2. Risk Management

    Can you identify and manage hazards during instruction?

  • 3. Teaching & Learning Strategies

    Can you communicate effectively and adapt your teaching style?

  • 4. Lesson Organisation

    Can you structure your lesson with clear aims and outcomes?

How to Prepare

This is where spending money on good coaching pays off. Part 3 preparation is worth every penny because this is the exam that trips most people up.

Recommended

ADI Coach / Trainer

1-on-1 coaching from someone who has passed Part 3 and teaches the PDI course. Expect to pay £500-800 for comprehensive preparation.

  • Mock exams with feedback
  • Personalised development plan
  • Techniques that work in real exams

Franchise Training Programme

Often included in franchise packages (£2,000-5,000). Good quality but expensive if you're going independent.

  • Structured course content
  • Group and 1-on-1 sessions
  • Expensive for what you get

Key Techniques to Master

Pupil-Centered Dialogue

Ask open questions, listen actively, avoid lecturing. "What do you think would happen if...?"

Start with Warm-Up, End with Review

Always start the lesson by establishing the pupil's current level and finish with what they'll take away.

The HAND Model

Hazard awareness, Anticipation, News (what's new), Driving (what to practice)

Verbal Commentary

Explain your thinking out loud so the pupil understands your decision-making process.

DRIVE DOJO TIP

My Part 3 experience: I failed my first attempt because I was teaching the way I thought the examiner wanted, not teaching the way a real pupil learns. On my second attempt, I pretended the examiner was genuinely nervous (they play this role well) and adapted my approach. I passed with flying colours.

What Sales Pitches Leave Out

You Pay Before You Earn

Joining fees and deposits are required before you're even qualified. If you fail Part 3, you've still paid — and you're not earning.

The Weekly Fee Never Stops

Even in slow periods — illness, holidays, bad weather, January-February slump — you still owe the fee. No pupils doesn't mean no payment.

You Build Their Brand

Every 5-star review, every referral, every word-of-mouth recommendation goes to the franchise. When you leave, you take nothing with you.

Contract Clawback Clauses

Many contracts require repayment of training costs if you leave within 3 years — sometimes £1,000–£3,000 on top of everything else.

DRIVE DOJO TIP

The math I did: Franchise fees of £200/week × 50 working weeks = £10,000/year in fees. With an independent school, that £10,000 stays in my pocket. Over 5 years, that's £50,000 I would have given away. The independent route is harder to start but dramatically more rewarding long-term.

CHAPTER 07

Setting Up Your Business

Once you're qualified, you need to set up your business properly. This chapter covers everything from registration to your first day of lessons.

Step 1: Register as an ADI

After passing Part 3, you must register with the DVSA. Your ADI registration lasts 4 years and must be renewed.

1

Apply Online

Apply through the DVSA website within 12 months of passing Part 3

2

Pay Registration Fee

£298 for 4-year registration

3

Receive Your Badge

Green ADI badge to display in your car

Step 2: Choose Your Business Structure

Recommended for Most

Sole Trader

The simplest structure. You are the business. Income is taxed as personal income.

  • Simplest to set up
  • Register with HMRC
  • Self Assessment tax return
  • Keep all profits

Limited Company

More complex. Suitable when income exceeds £40–50k annually.

  • Potential tax efficiencies
  • Separate legal entity
  • More admin
  • Accountancy costs

Most new ADIs should start as sole trader and switch later if needed.

Step 3: Register as Self-Employed

gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment

Registration Checklist:

  • Register as soon as you start earning — even on a trainee licence
  • Your first tax return covers registration date to April 5th
  • Set a reminder for January 31st deadline annually
  • Keep ALL receipts: fuel, car maintenance, insurance, advertising, phone, uniform

Step 4: Get the Right Insurance

Standard car insurance does NOT cover paid driving instruction. You need specialist ADI insurance.

Essential

Hire & Reward

This covers you when carrying passengers for payment. Without this, your claim will be rejected.

Dual Controls

Must be declared. Some insurers charge more, some don't — shop around.

Public Liability

Recommended. Protects you if a pupil injures themselves or damages property.

Recommended Providers:

Marmalade Adrian Flux Devitt Insurance Quote Me Happy LDG Insurance

Step 5: Your Car Setup

Dual Controls

Must be fitted by a specialist. Recommended companies:

Auto Clutch Systems (ACS) Driving School Supplies Bill Plant (if franchise)

Cost: £300–£500 depending on car model

Car Signage

Magnetic signs and roof box are your most cost-effective advertising. Magnetic signs can be removed when not working.

Magnetic Signs £30–£60
Roof Sign Box £50–£100
Vistaprint / Local £80–£150 total

What I did: Started with just magnetic signs (£40 from Vistaprint) and a laminated card in my window with my phone number. No roof box until I could afford it. The car looked professional enough to get my first pupils. Don't wait until everything is perfect — start as soon as you're legal.

My Launch Strategy: The Attract Offer

When I launched Drive Dojo, I needed to build my diary fast. Here's exactly what I did:

£49 First 2 Hours

Not just "first lesson half price" — a concrete, easy-to-advertise figure that made the decision simple for potential pupils.

  • Easy to understand
  • Feels like a deal
  • Commits them to 2 hours (sunk cost)
  • After that, normal rate applies

Result: 8 pupils in my first 2 weeks. After the attract lesson, 6 of them booked packages.

Packages vs. Hourly — What's Better?

Course Packages

  • Guaranteed income — you know what you're earning
  • Better diary management — lesson slots are filled
  • Structured progression — pupils commit to learning properly
  • Usually 5-10% discount — worth it for the commitment
Example: 10 hours @ £40/hr = £400
Package price: £360

Pay-As-You-Go

  • Flexible for pupils
  • No financial commitment required
  • Harder to manage diary
  • Pupils may cancel last-minute
Good for: Taster lessons, nervous beginners

My recommendation: Offer BOTH. Hourly for the first lesson (low commitment), then offer a package as the natural next step. Make the package feel like an investment, not just a discount.

CHAPTER 12

Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

One of the most valuable things I can offer is what NOT to do. Here are the mistakes I made and see other ADIs making repeatedly.

Underpricing to "Get Pupils"

The mistake: Charging £25/hour because you think low prices will attract pupils.

Result: You're working constantly but barely covering costs. You resent every lesson.

Fix: Price at market rate. £40-45/hour starting. Offer an attract offer, not permanent discounting.

No Cancellation Policy

The mistake: Letting pupils cancel last-minute or no-show without consequence.

Result: Wasted slots, lost income, resentment building.

Fix: State a 48-hour cancellation policy clearly from the first enquiry. Stick to it.

Not Saving for Tax

The mistake: Spending everything because "it's mine" without setting aside tax money.

Result: Tax bill arrives, you can't pay it. Penalties and stress.

Fix: Set up a standing order to move 25% of every payment to a savings account.

No Diary Management

The mistake: Accepting every booking as it comes without thinking about efficiency.

Result: Lessons scattered across the area, wasted driving time, exhaustion.

Fix: Block lessons geographically. Morning zone, afternoon zone. Build your diary around efficient routes.

Ignoring Marketing

The mistake: Thinking "I'm qualified, pupils will come."

Result: Empty diary, desperation, potentially signing a franchise deal.

Fix: Marketing is part of your job. 30 minutes per day minimum. Consistent effort compounds.

Taking Every Pupil

The mistake: Accepting any enquiry because you're desperate for pupils.

Result: Difficult pupils drain your energy, bad reviews, questioning your career choice.

Fix: It's okay to say "I'm not the right fit." Protect your diary for good-fit pupils.

Not Building Reviews

The mistake: Waiting for satisfied pupils to leave reviews naturally.

Result: 2 reviews when you need 20. No social proof. Enquiries don't convert.

Fix: Ask every passing pupil within 24 hours. Send them a direct link. Make it easy.

No Boundaries

The mistake: Available 7 days a week, replying to messages at 10pm, always saying yes.

Result: Burnout. Lesson quality suffers. No personal life.

Fix: Set clear working hours. Stick to them. Your business, your rules.
